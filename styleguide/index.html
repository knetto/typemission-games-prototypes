Flow Pattern

Full Game Flow

New games should reuse the same pacing: clear briefing, hardware reveal, active loop, immediate feedback and a compact result screen.

Intro3 briefing steps.
RevealShow the device and stats.
PlayTarget, input, progress.
FeedbackGreen correct, red mistake.
ResultCards, stamp, continue.
screen sequence

Reusable game pacing

  • IntroUse the fixed 3-step briefing: object, base hand position, action preview.
  • RevealShow one main hardware object with stats around it, never a crowded explanation screen.
  • Play loopKeep target, input and progress visible at the same time. The player should not search for the next action.
  • FeedbackCorrect actions use green glow; current targets use orange or finger color; mistakes use red circles/shake.
  • EndscreenUse result cards plus one stamp/status line. Keep metrics compact and reuse the same card language across games.

door keypad anatomy

How the object is built

  • PurposeThe door is the threat and the keypad is the tool. The metal slab makes the mission feel physical, while the small terminal explains the current code target.
  • Door slabUse a vertical rounded rectangle with a 2-3px steel border and a grey gradient from #4a4d52 to #2a2d32. It should feel heavy, not shiny chrome.
  • RivetsFour small circular rivets sit near the corners. They make the door feel manufactured and give scale to the keypad module.
  • Security locksUse three circular status locks above the workbench. Green means still available/healthy, red means a mistake or failed lock. Keep them small and symbolic.
  • Panel bodyThe keypad panel is black hardware, usually #0d0e12, inset into the door. It needs an inner shadow so it feels bolted into the surface.
  • ScreenThe screen is tiny and functional: terminal black, purple/navy border, scanlines, system tags and slots. It shows the active code, not a paragraph of instructions.
  • KeysKeys are square, radius 6-8px, dark translucent fill, and active orange glow. The active key can pulse but must not change grid size.
  • CablesUse curved SVG wires in green, orange and red. They should look plugged into the rig, not like decorative lines in the background.
  • FooterSignal bars and port jacks sit in the panel footer. They add diagnostic realism and can animate gently during live typing.
  • StatusBottom door status uses one dot plus one word: locked, unlocked, failed. Red is locked/alarm, green is unlocked/success.
  • WhyThis object works because the UI reads as a real bypass device: metal shell, embedded screen, physical buttons, cables, diagnostics and a clear door state.

mechanical lock anatomy

Mechanical parts and why they look this way

  • PurposeThis game should feel more tactile and risky than keypad entry. The player is not pressing a device; they are manipulating a physical cylinder under pressure.
  • ThemeUse the red family: base #120505, terminal #140606, soft border #300f0f, crimson highlights and orange active states.
  • WorkbenchUse a black embedded panel like the keypad, but make the inner screen red and the cylinder area wider. The lock needs horizontal space for six chambers.
  • Code rowThe top screen still uses terminal slots. In lockpicking, orange slots mean pins are being aligned; final green means the full cylinder is cracked.
  • Progress stripSmall numbered cells 01-06 sit above the cylinder. They should show active, set and fault states without replacing the physical pin view.
  • Shear lineThe orange dashed horizontal line is the most important mechanical reference. It explains why a pin is correct: the driver/key-pin split aligns with it.
  • Pin chamberEach chamber is a narrow dark slot with inner shadow. It contains spring, driver pin and key pin. Keep the chamber border low until it becomes active.
  • SpringThe spring is a light metal coil above the driver pin. Compress it when picked; do not hide it, because it sells the mechanical action.
  • DriversStandard drivers are rectangular steel. Spool drivers use an hourglass center. Serrated drivers use dark grooves. These shapes make the lock feel varied.
  • Key pinKey pins are warm brass/orange and carry the typed letter. They are the player's contact point, so they are brighter than steel drivers.
  • ToolsThe pick is a long brushed-metal shape with a thin tip. The tension meter is vertical and can move through green, orange and red pressure states.
  • AnimationPin lift is a fast bounce. Wrong input shakes the active chamber. A solved cylinder can brighten and turn slightly, but avoid large layout movement.

fingerprint anatomy

Biometric scanner parts

  • PurposeThis object should feel precise, not brute force. The player fills biometric ridges and scan nodes until the fingerprint verifies.
  • ThemeUse gold/yellow as the scanner accent: base #1c1809, soft border #4a3e14, laser #ffcc00, success #9ad744.
  • BezelThe scanner is a dark inset rounded rectangle, radius about 16px. It needs a heavy inner shadow so the fingerprint sits behind glass.
  • GridUse a very small 8px gold scan grid inside the bezel. It should be subtle enough that the fingerprint remains readable.
  • FingerprintThe fingerprint begins grey/white at low opacity. Active ridge groups turn gold. Completed ridge groups turn green with glow.
  • LaserThe laser is a 3px horizontal sweep with a larger translucent glow band. It moves top to bottom and back over about 3s.
  • NodesMinutiae nodes are 18px circles with mono letter labels. Active nodes scale slightly and use a pulsing ring.
  • ScreenThe screen label should say biometric or scanner status, not generic hackcode. It uses gold text and the same slot system as the other door games.
  • SuccessOn success, stop the laser, turn ridge layers green, hide or fade scan nodes, and show the door status as unlocked.
  • WhyThe object belongs to the same family because it keeps the metal panel, screen, LEDs and ports, but swaps the input grid for a scanner core.

Motherboard and threat parts

  • PurposeThis game should feel like defending a machine, not unlocking a door. The central CPU is the protected object and the moving viruses are the threat.
  • Board baseUse dark green-black PCB colors: #020503, #091a0c, #15331c. Add a subtle radial cyan light near the CPU.
  • Reference layerBackground traces, chips, pads and vias should be very low opacity. They give depth without competing with active gameplay objects.
  • Active tracesLive data traces are thin blue/cyan lines over faint green base strokes. Compromised traces turn red and glow.
  • CPU packageThe CPU is square with a dark fiberglass substrate, gold edge contacts, corner notches, SMD caps and an outer steel retention clamp.
  • Heat spreaderThe IHS is brushed dark titanium with a 2.5px border and a tiny serial label. It should feel heavier than the green board below.
  • Core dieThe inner die is a small blue-glowing square labeled CPU. In attack state it becomes red and can read ERR.
  • Shield nodesShield or mistake nodes reuse the circular status language. Healthy is green, lost is red with a pop/shake.
  • VirusesVirus blobs are red circular SVG-like threats with spikes/rings and glitch brightness. Targeted viruses can turn orange.
  • PacketsDefense packets are small green circles with a white core. They travel along a trace and create a green explosion ring on hit.
  • AnimationKeep motherboard traces mostly static. Let viruses glitch, packets travel, LEDs pulse and compromised states flash red.
  • WhyThe visual reads as TypeMission because it keeps terminal colors, status language and hardware detail, but shifts the material from door metal to PCB electronics.

Terminal, conveyor and keyboard details

  • PurposeThis game is practice, so it should feel focused and readable. It still uses spy-tech hardware, but with less door threat and more training feedback.
  • TerminalUse a dark terminal panel with a clear header, ping/status label and small window controls. The header gives context without stealing focus.

Animation & Effects Rules

Animations should explain the object state. Use small repeated loops for live hardware, short one-shot feedback for input, and strong color changes only for success, danger or failure.

When to animate and how much

  • Idle loopsLEDs, signal bars, scanlines, cursors and lasers may loop because they imply live electronics. Keep opacity changes subtle.
  • Active targetOnly one current target should pulse strongly. Use orange for generic active, gold for fingerprint active, or finger color for training keyboard active.
  • CorrectCorrect feedback turns green, adds a glow, then settles. It can pop once, but should not continue bouncing.
  • WrongWrong feedback uses red plus a very short shake, usually 0.15-0.4s. Return to the exact same position after the shake.
  • ScanningScanner sweeps are horizontal lines moving vertically. Fingerprint uses gold and 3s; dossier scanning uses green, lower opacity and about 4.5s.
  • MechanicalLock pins move quickly with a small bounce. The movement should show spring pressure, not cartoon squash.
  • ProjectileCPU packets move along traces and end in a short expanding ring. The explosion is green because it represents defense, not damage.
  • AlarmAlarm states may flash red and vibrate the main object briefly. Do not keep the entire layout shaking for long periods.
  • GlareUse scanlines and glass glare on screens only. Do not place heavy glare over paper, metal plates or key labels.
  • ConsistencyEvery motion should answer one question: what is live, what is active, what succeeded, what failed, or what is under attack.
